## 5. Physical Interpretation

### 5.1 The Twin Asymptotic Behaviors

The Veneziano amplitude has two asymptotic regimes:
- **UV ($\mu \to \infty$):** $a \to 0$ — asymptotic freedom. The amplitude vanishes like $\Gamma(a) \sim 1/a$.
- **IR ($\mu \to 0$):** $a \to 1$ — Veneziano pole (tachyon). The amplitude diverges like $1/(1-a)$.

In QED:
- **UV:** $\alpha \to \infty$ — Landau pole. The coupling blows up.
- **IR:** $\alpha \to 1/137$ — weak coupling. Perturbation theory works.

The compactification must MAP the Veneziano UV ($a \to 0$, free) to the QED UV ($\alpha \to \infty$, strong), and the Veneziano IR ($a \to 1$, pole) to the QED IR ($\alpha \to 1/137$, weak).

### 5.2 The Inversion Mapping

$$a_{\text{Veneziano}} \longleftrightarrow \alpha_{\text{QED}}$$

$$\text{UV: } a \to 0 \longleftrightarrow \alpha \to \infty$$

$$\text{IR: } a \to 1 \longleftrightarrow \alpha \to 1/137$$

This requires a mapping $\alpha(a)$ that is:
1. **Inverting:** Maps large $a$ (Veneziano IR) to small $\alpha$ (QED weak coupling)
2. **Stretching:** Provides the factor $S \approx 3,240$ in RG time
3. **Geometric:** Emerges from Calabi-Yau intersection numbers

### 5.3 Candidate Mapping

A natural candidate is:

$$\alpha(a) = \frac{\alpha_0}{(1-a)^n}$$

where $n$ is determined by the compactification. At the symmetric point:

$$\alpha(0.5) = \frac{\alpha_0}{0.5^n} = 2^n \alpha_0$$

Setting $\alpha(0.5) \approx 1$ (strong coupling at unification):

$$2^n \approx 137 \quad \Rightarrow \quad n \approx 7.1$$

This suggests $n$ could be related to the number of compact dimensions (6 or 7 for Calabi-Yau).
