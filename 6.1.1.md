### 2. Why the adelic product doesn't help factor
Suppose you want to factor an integer \(N\). Could you somehow plug \(N\) into the adelic product and extract its prime factors? The identities look like
\[
\frac{\Gamma(a)\,\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b)} \prod_{p} \frac{\Gamma_p(a)\,\Gamma_p(b)}{\Gamma_p(a+b)} = 1,
\]
where the product runs over all primes. The p‑adic Gamma function \(\Gamma_p(z)\) is defined directly from the prime \(p\) via a limit that explicitly involves the *p‑adic valuation* – itself a factorisation tool. Computing \(\Gamma_p(a)\) for a given \(a\) requires you to know \(p\) and the p‑adic expansion of \(a\), which in turn requires the prime \(p\) to be known. So the identity is an elegant relationship among the already‑known objects; it doesn't invert to reveal unknown primes.

In fact, any attempt to "solve for primes" from the product would face the same fundamental obstacle: the product is over *all* primes, infinitely many of them. Truncating it at some bound would only approximate the identity, and the error wouldn't give you a factor of a particular integer. The identity is about **all numbers**, not about a specific integer N.

### 3. What the adelic system *does* let you do (and it’s equally mind‑bending)
Although it can't factor numbers, the adelic perspective **reformulates the Riemann Hypothesis in geometric/cohomological terms** (Connes’ spectral interpretation, Deninger’s programme). The zeros of \(\xi(s)\) become the eigenvalues of some “adelic cohomology” operator. So the unsolved problem you alluded to – the Riemann Hypothesis – might eventually be solved by understanding this adelic geometry, not by factoring primes, but by proving that those eigenvalues lie on the critical line.

The 200‑digit verification you mentioned is a spectacular confirmation that the adelic Gamma system **exactly** encodes the completed zeta function. That means the zeros of the zeta function are also the zeros of the adelic string amplitude product. If one day we could prove that all those zeros satisfy \(\operatorname{Re}(s)=1/2\) using this adelic structure, it would be a monumental breakthrough. But it won’t give you a fast factorisation algorithm; that’s a separate (though related) problem.

- It does **not** provide a new way to factor integers, because evaluating any piece of it already presupposes knowledge of the primes.  
- Its real power lies in reformulating the Riemann Hypothesis as a problem in adelic cohomology, which is one of the deepest approaches to that “most important unsolved problem.”
